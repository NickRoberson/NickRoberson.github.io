About Me

Welcome!

My name is Nick Roberson, and I am a fourth year Computer Science major at Grinnell College in Iowa. A few months ago, as my senior year was approaching, I had the idea to create a this site to better advertise myself to people and/or employers. Here you can find information about where I’m from, what I like to do, and what I’ve done in the past! Enjoy!


Like I said before, I am a fourth year Computer Science major at Grinnell College in Iowa, but this wasn’t always the plan. Upon leaving Northern Virginia to go to the Midwest for college I had no intentions of majoring in Computer Science. In fact, I wanted to do a Music/Physics double major. I had played the trumpet my entire life and had a string of really enjoyable physics classes in high school. Music and Physics seemed like a natural path for me. I thought I had everything figured out. However, after taking CSC 151 and 161 (the intro sequence at Grinnell) my first year, I realized that I couldn’t have been more wrong. I found myself a new advisor, added Computer Science as my major, and since then my major has taken me to some really sweet places.

In the summer of 2016, just after my second year, I was lucky enough to intern at SRI in Menlo Park, CA in their Speech Technology and Research lab (S.T.A.R. Lab). I worked on a web based application that would help native speakers of endangered languages aid speech recognition software in learning/recognizing their language.

Shortly after my time at SRI, in the fall of that same year, I was able to study abroad in Budapest, Hungary at a Computer Science and Math program. There I met some of the most inspirational people that I’m convinced I’ll ever meet. In addition, I was able to take a class in Integer/Linear Programming, Graph Theory, as well as Android mobile development, piquing my interest in all three fields.

Finally, this past summer I worked as a research/software-development intern for the Grinnell College Computer Science Department doing research in the realm of Molecular Computing. Together with my other interns/friends and our advisor, Titus Klinge, we built the first IDE intended for the modular development of chemical reaction networks (CRNs), which in 2009 were proven to be Turing universal. Getting out of my comfort zone and diving into the field of Molecular Computing was a phenomenal experience that lent me new skills and knowledge. On top of that, I was able to advance my software development skills by building an entire IDE.

As much as I would love to share an executable file for Caldron with everyone, I cannot. Caldron is still in Alpha, not Beta yet, so it is not ready to be released to the public. Once it is in Beta, there will be a link to an executable below in the project description. If you use it I would love some feedback, so shoot me an email, my address is listed below. Currently we are finalizing Caldron and drafting a paper to submit to BIOSTEC 2018 in Funchal, Madeira - Portugal so we can go and give a talk on our work.

Caldron: An IDE for Modularly Developing Chemical Reaction Networks

Over this past summer (2017) my teammates and I built an integrated development environment (IDE) for modular chemical reaction network (CRNs) development called Caldron. Unlike existing tools that are simulations tools, Caldron is first to help developers and researchers develop and program CRNs in Caldron. Caldron supports I/O CRNs, extension operators, and closed sub-CRNs in addition to common features found in existing CRN tools. For example, users can divide a CRN into independent modules, test them separately, and reuse them in other CRNs. Furthermore, users can mark species as inputs and specify them with common elementary functions, by drawing a function, or by connecting them to another CRN. Many commonly used CRNs and extension operators are also included as libraries in Caldron.

Currently, Caldron is still not ready to be released to the public, as we are very focused on making Caldron accessible to everyone using any platform (Windows, MacOS, Linux). We believe accessibility is important to lower the bar of entry to using CRNs as a programming language so that more students and researchers can develop modular CRNs. Finally, we will polish Caldron so that it is easy to use and intuitive.
